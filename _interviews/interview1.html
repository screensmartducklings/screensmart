Q1- What is your opinion about screentime management? where do you draw the line between healthy and unhealthy usage?

A1- As a mother of two young kids, I believe managing screen time is essential, especially in today’s world where screens are everywhere. It’s not that I’m completely against screen time—I understand that it can have educational benefits and can be a way to unwind. However, the line between healthy and unhealthy usage comes down to balance, content quality, and how screens are integrated into our daily lives.

For me, healthy usage means:

  • Limited time per day: I allow my children to use screens for about 30 minutes to an hour a day, depending on the circumstances. This gives them enough time to enjoy digital content but prevents overexposure.
  • Quality content: I make sure that the apps or videos they use are educational, age-appropriate, and ideally interactive. I avoid passive content like endless cartoons or videos with no learning value.
  • No screen time during meals or before bed: This helps us maintain family bonding moments and ensures that their sleep isn’t affected by overstimulation from screens.

Unhealthy usage would be:

  • Excessive hours: When screen time extends beyond an hour or becomes something they demand throughout the day, it starts to concern me.
  • Mindless content consumption: If they’re watching random, fast-paced, or overly commercial content, I see it as unhealthy. I try to steer away from shows or games with no educational value or those that promote consumerism.
  • Using screens as a pacifier: I avoid using screens as a quick solution to tantrums or boredom. I believe that over-relying on screens to manage behavior can become a slippery slope.

Ultimately, it’s about creating a balanced approach where screens are one part of their day, but not a dominant one. I try to involve them in outdoor play, hands-on activities, and social interaction as much as possible to build healthy habits.

Q2- Can you share some examples on how you have managed to get your children to obey your screentime limits?

A2- Managing screen time limits with young children definitely requires patience and consistency, but over time, we’ve developed some strategies that work well for our family. Here are a few examples of how I’ve managed to get my kids to respect those boundaries:

  1. Setting clear expectations and routines

One of the first things I did was establish a clear routine around screen time. I’ve made it known that screens are allowed only after certain activities are completed, like finishing homework, playing outside, or doing a craft project. This way, the kids know what to expect and don’t ask for screens randomly throughout the day.

  • For example, I tell them: “After we finish lunch and play outside for an hour, you can have 30 minutes of screen time.” By sticking to this pattern, they understand that screen time is a reward, not a default activity.
  1. Offering alternative activities

I always make sure there are interesting alternatives to screens available, so they don’t see it as the only option for fun. I’ve noticed that when kids are bored, they gravitate toward screens, so I keep a variety of toys, books, and craft supplies readily available.

  • For instance, I might say, “We can watch a show later, but why don’t we do some coloring or build a fort first?” Giving them exciting alternatives helps reduce their desire to turn to screens automatically.
  1. Using a timer to reinforce limits

To avoid conflicts about when screen time should end, I’ve started using a timer. I set it for the agreed-upon screen time—say 30 minutes—and when it rings, it signals that screen time is over. This makes the process less about me enforcing the limit and more about the timer doing it, which helps reduce resistance.

  • I’ll say, “When the timer goes off, it’s time to turn off the tablet. Then we can bake cookies together!” Tying the end of screen time to a fun next activity helps smooth the transition.
  1. Screen-free family time

We make an effort to have “screen-free” times as a family, where we all put away our devices and do something together. Whether it’s reading, cooking, or playing board games, the kids see that even the adults take breaks from screens, which helps reinforce the idea that screens aren’t the most important thing.

  • On weekends, I’ll say, “Let’s have a no-screen morning! We can go to the park or do a puzzle together.” This way, they see that family time is more valuable than individual screen time.
  1. Praise and positive reinforcement

When my kids follow the screen time rules without fuss, I make sure to praise them for it. Positive reinforcement really helps them feel good about following the limits. I’ll acknowledge their good behavior and sometimes give them a small reward, like an extra bedtime story or a special snack.

  • I’ll say, “I love how you turned off the tablet right when the timer went off! You did such a great job following the rules.”
  1. Choosing screen time content together

To make screen time feel less like something they’re fighting for and more like a shared activity, I often let them help choose what they’ll watch or play—but within boundaries I set. This way, they feel a sense of control but also learn to make good choices.

  • For example, I might say, “We can pick one learning game or a nature documentary to watch. Which one sounds fun to you?” This gives them ownership while ensuring the content is appropriate.

By combining clear expectations, alternative activities, and positive reinforcement, I’ve found that my children generally respect the screen time limits. It’s not always perfect, but consistency has really helped in the long run!

Q3- When you do not have the time to participate in an activity with your kids, what do you do to make sure they are not bored, and they resist the urge to watch videos all day?

A3- As a mother, there are definitely times when I can’t be fully involved in an activity with my kids, whether it’s because I’m cooking, cleaning, or handling other responsibilities around the house. During those times, I’ve developed a few strategies to keep them engaged and entertained without turning to screens all day. Here’s how I manage it:

  1. Setting up independent play zones

I’ve created spaces in our home where my kids can engage in independent play. These zones have toys, books, and activities that I know they enjoy and can handle on their own. I rotate toys and activities to keep things fresh, so they don’t get bored with the same options.

  • For example, one corner might have building blocks and puzzles, while another has art supplies like crayons, paper, and stickers. By rotating activities, they’re more likely to stay entertained for longer periods.
  1. Encouraging imaginative play

Imaginative play is a huge part of how I keep them busy when I can’t be directly involved. I encourage them to play pretend—whether it’s being superheroes, running a “store,” or caring for their stuffed animals. I’ve noticed that when they’re immersed in pretend play, they can keep themselves occupied for long stretches.

  • I might give them a suggestion, like “Why don’t you set up a little café with your toy kitchen?” or “How about you put on a show with your puppets?” This sparks their creativity and helps them get started on their own.
  1. Introducing structured activities

When I know I’ll be busy for a while, I often set up structured activities that can keep them engaged independently. Craft projects, simple science experiments, or sensory play are great options that don’t require constant supervision but still feel exciting to them.

  • I might give them a craft kit with instructions to follow, or I’ll set up a sensory bin filled with rice, toy animals, and cups for scooping. They can explore these activities on their own while I’m working nearby.
  1. Using audio content as a screen-free alternative

When I really need to keep them occupied and I don’t want them watching videos, I often use audiobooks or kid-friendly podcasts. This is a great way for them to engage their imagination without being glued to a screen.

  • I’ll say, “How about we listen to a fun story while I finish cooking?” We have a few favorite audiobooks, and it’s something they can do together while still being entertained. They might even act out parts of the story as they listen.
  1. Creating mini “missions” or challenges

To add an element of excitement, I sometimes give them small “missions” or challenges that can keep them busy for a while. These can be scavenger hunts around the house, building challenges with their toys, or setting up little obstacle courses.

  • For example, I might say, “Can you find five red things in the house?” or “Let’s see if you can build the tallest tower with your blocks.” This gives them a goal to work toward and distracts them from the idea of watching videos.
  1. Engaging them in household tasks

When appropriate, I try to involve them in what I’m doing, even if it’s a simple task. Kids love to feel helpful, so I give them small jobs that they can handle, and this keeps them engaged while I get things done.

  • If I’m cooking, I might give them a plastic bowl and spoon to “mix” with or ask them to help me sort vegetables. If I’m folding laundry, I might ask them to fold their own small items or match socks. This makes them feel involved and keeps them busy.
  1. Implementing a reward system for screen-free time

To encourage them to stay away from screens, I’ve sometimes used a reward system. For example, if they play independently or engage in non-screen activities for a certain amount of time, they can earn a small reward, like extra playtime outside or choosing a favorite story for bedtime.

  • I’ll say, “If you play nicely on your own for 30 minutes, we can have an extra story at bedtime.” This helps them build the habit of enjoying non-screen activities and gives them something to look forward to.

By creating these opportunities for independent, imaginative, or structured play, I’ve found that my kids are much less likely to rely on screens when I’m not able to directly engage with them. It’s all about giving them the tools to entertain themselves in meaningful and fun ways!

Q4­- Has there been a time, when your kids discovered really addictive types of video content and engaged mostly with those, what did you do to stop them from consuming such media?

A4- You’re absolutely right—children, especially at ages 5 to 7, don’t necessarily understand what is “good” or “bad” for them. They respond to immediate feelings, and watching content like unboxing videos or toy reviews is designed to be very entertaining and addictive. Simply telling them that it’s not good for them won’t stop them from wanting to watch more. With that in mind, I’ve used more practical approaches to manage the situation when my kids have been hooked on this kind of content. Here’s what I did that worked:

  1. Gradually Reducing Access Instead of Cutting it Off

Instead of immediately banning the addictive content, I reduced how often they watched it. Going cold turkey usually results in tantrums and makes them crave it even more. I began by allowing it only for short, designated times, then gradually cutting down on those times.

  • For example, I’d say: “You can watch one video after dinner, but then we’re going to do something else.” Slowly, I decreased the frequency to every other day and then to weekends only.
  1. Replacing the Content with Equally Engaging Alternatives

Children are driven by entertainment, so I focused on finding alternatives that could compete with the excitement of those unboxing videos. I introduced interactive, engaging apps and games that were still fun but had some educational value or creativity involved.

  • For instance, I introduced apps where they could build things, like a simple Lego game, or a drawing appwhere they could create their own stories. By letting them actively participate rather than passively watching, they were still entertained but with healthier options.
  1. Using a Reward System

I found that creating a reward system helped to curb the desire for the addictive content. I told them they could earn extra privileges, like a special snack or a trip to the park, if they spent their screen time on educational content or non-screen activities.

  • I’d say, “If you watch something else today, like a learning game, or play outside for 30 minutes instead of watching YouTube, we’ll do something fun together later.” The key was to make the alternative seem exciting and rewarding.
  1. Incorporating Videos They Like Into Playtime

Rather than fighting their love for these videos, I incorporated the theme into their off-screen play. If they were into toy unboxing or watching other kids play, I encouraged them to mimic it with their own toys, essentially turning the videos into an inspiration for real-life play.

  • I might say, “Let’s pretend we’re making our own unboxing video! You can open this new toy and tell me all about it.” This way, they get the thrill of the unboxing experience, but in a hands-on, screen-free format.
  1. Creating a “Video Playlist” to Control Content

Another approach that worked was making a “video playlist” of pre-approved content. I’d allow them to watch videos, but only the ones I chose in advance. I created a playlist of educational shows or creative storytelling videos on YouTube, limiting access to the more addictive content.

  • I’d say, “Here’s a new playlist we can watch together! It’s about animals and cool experiments.” This way, I controlled the flow of content while still giving them the impression they were getting to watch videos.
  1. Setting Clear Boundaries With a Visual Timer

I realized that kids this age respond better to something concrete, so I used a visual timer or clock to let them know when their screen time was ending. When the timer goes off, they know it’s time to stop. This avoids the constant back-and-forth of “just one more video.”

  • I’d set a timer for 20 minutes and say, “When the timer goes off, we’re all done with screens for today. Then we can play with your new toys or go outside.” By consistently sticking to the timer, they eventually stopped fighting it because it became predictable.
  1. Involving Them in Creating “Screen-Free” Plans

Kids love feeling like they have some control over their day, so I involved them in planning fun, screen-free activities ahead of time. By letting them help plan, they felt more invested in these non-screen alternatives.

  • I’d ask, “What should we do after screen time today? Should we bake cookies or do a craft?” Giving them the choice made them excited about what was coming next, rather than dreading the end of their video time.
  1. Being Consistent and Staying Patient

One of the hardest but most important things I did was to stay consistent. Even when they pushed back, whined, or tried to negotiate, I stuck to the limits I set. It wasn’t always easy, but over time they got used to the new routine, especially when they knew that I wasn’t going to cave in.

  • I found that patience and repetition were key. After a few weeks of sticking to the limits and offering alternatives, the addiction to those specific videos lessened, and they became more open to other types of content or activities.

By making the process gradual, offering exciting alternatives, and using visual cues like timers and rewards, I found a way to break the habit without making it a constant battle. The goal was to redirect their attention toward healthier activities while still respecting their love of entertainment.

Q5- Children learn from their parents, and therefore it is important that parents model blood behaviour. Did you and your husband have to make any changes to the way you use screens?

A5- You’re right—repetition can be avoided to keep things fresh. Here’s a refined version of how we, as parents, adjusted our screen habits without overlapping previous points:

 

  1. Practicing “Phone-Free” Zones During Key Family Times

One of the most effective changes we made was establishing **specific times and areas of the house where screens were off-limits** for everyone, not just the kids. For example, during breakfast and dinner, both my husband and I made sure our phones were completely out of sight—either left in another room or turned off. This ensured that we were fully present during important family moments.

- This helped show the kids that meals and family time are for connection, not distractions, and created a natural pause in screen use for all of us.

 

  1. Shifting to Productive Screen Use

While we couldn’t eliminate screens altogether—especially with my husband’s work and the occasional need to check emails—we both consciously started shifting our screen use toward more **productive tasks**. Instead of scrolling through social media or watching random videos, we started using screens for meaningful things, like reading articles or learning new skills.

- We would explain, “Mommy’s using the phone to read a recipe for dinner,” or “Daddy’s looking up something to build with you later.” This helped reinforce that screens aren’t just for entertainment—they can be a tool for learning or doing productive activities.

 

  1. Using Screens as a Tool for Togetherness

Instead of always treating screens as something that separated us, we started using them as a way to **connect with each other**. For example, we would occasionally find a family-friendly video tutorial to follow, like learning a new recipe or a craft project together.

- We’d say, “Let’s use the tablet to watch a video on how to make these cool paper airplanes!” By framing screen time as something we could enjoy together and that had a tangible result, we showed the kids that screens can be a source of positive interaction.

 

  1. Being Transparent About Our Own Struggles

We made a point of being honest with our kids about how hard it can be to manage screen time as adults. If one of us realized we had spent too much time on our phone or felt distracted, we talked openly about it. For example, I’d say, “I’m putting my phone away now because I don’t want to be on it all day.”

- This helped our children see that managing screen time is something even adults need to be mindful of, and that it’s okay to recognize when it’s becoming too much. It also encouraged them to reflect on their own habits.

 

  1. Scheduling Intentional Downtime Without Screens

Instead of filling every free moment with screens, we made a deliberate effort to schedule **screen-free downtime** for ourselves as well as the kids. This might be a 30-minute window in the evening when we all unwind without any devices—reading a book, going for a walk, or simply relaxing without distractions.

- I’d say, “Let’s all take some quiet time—no phones, no tablets, just relax or read a book.” Creating this as a family ritual helped normalize the idea that screens aren’t needed for every part of the day.

 

  1. Sharing the Purpose Behind Screen Use

Whenever we did have to use screens—whether for work or something important—we started being clear about **why** we were using them. If I was on my laptop for work, I’d tell the kids, “I need to finish some work now, but once I’m done, I’ll put it away, and we’ll play together.” This transparency helped them understand the distinction between necessary screen time and entertainment.

- My husband would also say, “I’m checking an email now, and then I’m done with my phone for the day.” This made screen use purposeful rather than mindless, setting an example of how screens can be a tool, not just a habit.

 

  1. Switching to Physical Alternatives

Whenever we noticed we were spending too much time on screens for activities we could do in another way—like reading news on our phones or checking social media—we started replacing those habits with **physical alternatives**. For instance, instead of reading the news on a device, we’d occasionally pick up a physical newspaper or magazine, showing the kids that not all information comes from screens.

- We’d say, “Let’s look through this nature magazine together,” or “We can read a real book tonight instead of watching TV.” This change helped highlight that there are plenty of ways to enjoy content without relying on screens.

 

  1. Limiting Multi-Screening

We made a conscious effort to **avoid using multiple screens at once**—like having the TV on while also scrolling through phones. Multi-tasking with screens is something we realized could set a bad example, as it encourages passive consumption and constant distraction.

- If we were watching a show or movie, we made sure not to use our phones at the same time. This simple change showed the kids that it’s important to focus on one thing at a time, and it made our screen use more intentional.

 

By making these changes to our own habits—like reducing mindless screen use, being transparent, and setting clear boundaries—we modeled the behaviors we wanted to see in our children. This approach didn’t just teach them to limit their own screen time; it helped us build a healthier family dynamic around how we all use screens.

Q6- Finally, what would you like to say to our readers? who want to achieve the same? What advice do you have for them? (Your response should include: how the parents should be steadfastand patient, how they should keep educating themselves around this topic and adapting to their child's unique needs and conditions and how they can get started on managing screens if their children are already addicted and have unhealthy screentime.)

A6- To all the parents out there who want to manage their children’s screen time in a healthier way, I want to say this: be steadfast and patient, but also flexible and understanding. Changing screen habits, especially if your child is already addicted, is not easy. It takes time, consistency, and a lot of trial and error. However, it’s absolutely possible, and your efforts will pay off in the long run. Here are a few key pieces of advice to help you along the way:

  1. Be Patient and Consistent

Changing screen habits doesn’t happen overnight. It’s important to be patient with your children—and with yourself. Kids often resist change, especially when it comes to something as engaging as screens. You might face pushback, tantrums, or frustration, but remain consistent with the limits and rules you set.

  • Start with small changes, like reducing screen time gradually or setting clear screen-free times, and stick to them. Even if your child protests at first, over time they will adjust to the new routine.
  1. Educate Yourself and Stay Adaptable

The digital world is constantly evolving, and so are our children’s needs. As parents, it’s important to keep educating ourselves on the latest trends in technology, digital media, and child development. Understanding the content your child is exposed to, and how it affects their behavior, will help you make informed decisions about what’s appropriate and what isn’t.

  • Read up on the latest research about screen time, talk to other parents, and consult professionals if needed. What works for one child may not work for another, so be prepared to adapt your approach based on your child’s unique needs and personality.
  1. Don’t Demonize Screens—Find Balance

It’s easy to see screens as the enemy, but they aren’t inherently bad. The key is balance. Instead of banning screens outright, focus on integrating them into your child’s life in a healthy way. Allow some screen time, but ensure it’s used for age-appropriate, educational, or creative activities.

  • Set clear boundaries: establish limits on when and where screens can be used, and try to prioritize other activities, like outdoor play, reading, and family time. The goal is to create a healthy relationship with screens, not to eliminate them completely.
  1. Model the Behavior You Want to See

Children learn by observing their parents. If they see you constantly on your phone or watching TV, they’ll want to do the same. Be mindful of your own screen habits and make an effort to model healthy behavior.

  • Set aside your devices during family time, be present in the moment, and show your children that screens aren’t the center of your attention. This will help them understand that screens are just one part of life, not the most important thing.
  1. Start Slowly if Your Child is Already Addicted

If your child is already addicted to screens, don’t panic. Start by setting small, manageable goals rather than trying to implement drastic changes all at once. Begin by introducing screen-free times during certain parts of the day, such as during meals or before bedtime, and slowly build from there.

  • Gradually replace screen time with other engaging activities, like crafts, outdoor play, or reading. Involve your child in these activities to make the transition smoother and more enjoyable for them. Over time, they will become more accustomed to spending time away from screens.
  1. Communicate and Involve Your Child

Talk to your child about the changes you want to make and explain why it’s important. Involve them in setting new routines and finding alternative activities. When children feel like they have a say, they’re more likely to cooperate.

  • For younger children, keep the explanations simple, like “Too much screen time isn’t good for your brain, so we’re going to have more time to play and do fun things together.” For older children, help them understand the long-term impact on their health and well-being.
  1. Be Ready for Setbacks

There will be days when things don’t go as planned, and that’s okay. You might allow more screen time during a busy day or when your child is sick or tired, and that’s perfectly fine. Don’t be too hard on yourself or your child. What matters is the overall pattern you’re creating—a gradual shift towards healthier habits.

  • If things get off track, don’t give up. Recommit to the plan and keep moving forward. Remember that progress takes time, and small changes will add up over time.
  1. Celebrate the Wins

As you make progress, celebrate the small victories. If your child successfully reduces their screen time or participates in more non-screen activities, acknowledge and praise them. Positive reinforcement helps children feel good about their progress and motivates them to keep going.

  • Whether it’s a special family outing or just extra playtime together, celebrating these moments will create positive associations with reducing screen time.

In conclusion, managing screen time effectively is a journey that requires patience, education, and adaptability. Start slowly, set clear boundaries, and model the behavior you want to see. It’s important to find a balance that works for your family and remember that the goal is not perfection but progress. Stay consistent, stay informed, and trust that with time, you can help your child develop healthier screen habits.
