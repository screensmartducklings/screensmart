Understanding Ratings and Safe Practices

In a world where children are increasingly immersed in digital media, you must guide them through the complexities of online gaming, social media, and digital content. But first, you need to get familiar with different kinds of ratings and learn how to read different symbols.

Ratings in the Gaming World + Apps

  1. Entertainment Software Rating Board (ESRB) Ratings

ESRB ratings provide information about what’s in a game or app so parents and consumers can make informed choices about which games are right for their family. They offer different kinds of ratings, namely: Rating Categories, Content Descriptors, and Interactive Elements. They also have an app for Rating Summaries where more detailed information is provided.

  1. Pan European Game Information (PEGI) Ratings

PEGI provides age classifications for video games in 38 European countries. The age rating confirms that the game content is appropriate for players of a certain age. PEGI considers the age suitability of a game, not the level of difficulty.

Rating Systems in the World of TV and Movies

  1. TV Parental Guidelines

The television industry designed a TV ratings system – known as the TV Parental Guidelines – to give parents more information about the content and age-appropriateness of TV programs.

   
  1. Motion Pictures Association’s Rating System

Established in 1968, the film rating system provides parents with the information needed to determine if a film is appropriate for their children.

 

Who They’re Playing With

Now that you’ve understood the different types of rating systems and what they mean, have regular conversations with your child about who they’re playing with. MMO Game or MMOG (Massive Multi-player Online Game) is a kind of game with large numbers of players on the same server. This means that it isn’t always necessary that our child is playing with someone he/she knows in real life. While most games have rules and privacy settings, you should be aware of who your child’s online friends are.

Depending on your child’s age and the game they’re playing you can set a rule or encourage them to play online with relatives and known friends.

Navigating Online Content

  • Privacy Settings: Review social media privacy settings together. And teach older kids and teens how to enable privacy settings. If someone unknown is contacting your child, make sure your child is able to approach you about it. Teach them how they can block people online.
  • Personal Information: Stress the importance of not sharing personal details online. And advise against sharing location details and personal information.
  • Open Communication
    • Discuss both the benefits and risks of social media.
    • Encourage your child to share their online experiences with you.
  • Posting Content and Comments
    • Teach your child to think before posting and understand the potential reactions.
    • Remind them to seek consent before sharing images or information about others.
  • Parental Controls
    • Most games and apps have parental control settings, you can search the web for more details.
    • Gaming consoles have time limits and other settings that you can configure that suits you and your child the best.
  • Staying Involved
    • Engagement: Play games with your child or watch them play, asking about their experiences.
    • Device Placement: Make sure they’re playing or viewing media in a place with foot traffic like the family room.
  • Unexpected Encounters
    • Prepare your child to deal with accidental exposure to harmful content.
    • Encourage critical thinking about what they encounter online.

Conclusion

In the digital age, parenting involves navigating the complex world of online games, social media, and digital content. By understanding rating systems like ESRB, PEGI, MPA, and TV Parental Guidelines, and engaging in open conversations about online safety, screen time, and digital footprint, parents can effectively guide their children through the digital landscape. Remember, the key is not to restrict but to educate and empower your child to make informed decisions in the digital world.
